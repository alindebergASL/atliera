## 1. What M3 actually closed — the headline

**The Atliera doctrine loop is now visible end-to-end on a real ratified durable write.** The state on `main` at `b2b7a09` lets a reviewer run the demo and see:

- a real proposal (the curated public-source materialization input),
- a real approval packet (no-call, drafted),
- a real one-shot operator arming carrying exactly one attributable ratifier identity,
- a real durable graph-write executor that flipped `graph_ingestion_performed` and `durable_writes_performed` from false to true for the first time in the project's history, stamping `mediation_gate_level: "L0"` on exactly the write that happened,
- a real `tables/graph_snapshots.jsonl` row in the local durable DB carrying the M3 admission trust label `model-proposed-human-ratified-evidence-pending`,
- a real read-only snapshots reader that pulls that row back, descriptor-snapshots it with `util.types.isProxy` and deep-freezes the validated bundle,
- a real static HTML render that shows the ratified record under its honest M3 admission decoration and the per-record `Source-backed` provenance pill, alongside a review-decision rejection clearly framed as "Not in graph",
- and the committed demo HTML at `fixtures/workshop/workshop-public-proposal-durable-state-render-demo.html` regenerated by `scripts/generate-3b-demo.mts` from that real 3a write — not hand-authored.

Every doctrine boundary M3 was meant to establish is now established by code, not by description:

> **Agent proposes → system validates → human ratifies → durable state**, with the durable state telling the truth about its own evidence status under the admission trust label, with the rejection sitting beside it clearly not in the graph.

The 3a retro's trust-tier discipline (row-level `trust_label` vs. per-record `provenance_status`, disagreeing only conservatively, with M4/M5b reserved for `verified` flips) is now enforced at three boundaries: the executor on the write side (refuses to stamp `verified` on records under an M3 trust label), the reader on the read side (refuses to surface a row that contradicts the contract — its own surfaced refusal code `row_bundle_marks_record_verified` fires before the bundle validator so the row-level invariant gets its own audit-trail line), and the render-side composer (a fail-closed re-assertion that throws `DurableStateRenderRefusal` even on a forged reader result). The doctrine is no longer a sentence in a runbook; it is a property the code defends.

## 2. The 3b hardening cycle — three rounds, and what they taught

The 3b slice did not merge on its first green-CI head. It went through three rounds of collaborator-found gap, empirical reproduction, hardening, and regression-locking before the trust boundary was sealed. The pattern is doctrinally significant and worth recording explicitly:

**Round 1 — the deep-freeze + decision-artifact gap (head `5ed3762`).** The reader returned a shallow-frozen row; the nested bundle, its arrays, and its records were mutable. A caller could flip a per-record `provenance_status` to `verified` AFTER the read boundary validated it, and the renderer would emit `trust-verified`. Verified empirically. In parallel, `composeDurableStateView` rendered `decisionArtifact.decisions` with no runtime validation, invoking getter-backed fields and rendering rejections from broadened/wrong-kind artifacts. Hardened at `f281ac0`: deep-freeze the validated bundle; re-assert the trust-tier invariant at the render boundary; introduce a render-side decision-artifact validator using the same `isProxy` + descriptor-snapshot discipline as the executor and reader.

**Round 2 — accessor-backed array indices + nested getters (head `f281ac0`).** A non-Proxy array with an accessor on `"0"` passed `Array.isArray` + `isProxy` checks but fired its getter on `decisionsRaw[i]`. Verified empirically. Separately, after `isProxy(row)` and `isProxy(row.bundle)` passed, `objectCardsFromBundle` read `obj.provenance_status` directly, firing a getter on the un-snapshotted record. Verified empirically. Hardened at `1150dc7`: a new `snapshotPlainArray` helper descriptor-snapshots arrays so accessor-backed indices are refused; durable rows are descriptor-snapshotted at compose entry so nested getters never fire.

**Round 3 — the root `readerResult` itself (head `1150dc7`).** `composeDurableStateView` read `readerResult.rows` directly from the top-level input before snapshotting. A getter installed on the root object's `.rows` fired. Verified empirically. Hardened at `f895fb4`: snapshot the root `readerResult` first; read every field from the snapshot, never from the input.

**The doctrinal lesson is the round count, not the fixes.** Each round closed a real bypass. Each round was found by trying the same shape of attack one layer deeper. The discipline `snapshot-and-revalidate-at-entry-for-every-control-flow-field` (the 3a retro §3 generalization) is not a thing the executor does once and the read path inherits — it is a property every trust boundary must re-establish for itself, recursively, every time it consumes a value it did not produce. That property is now defended at the executor (3a), the reader (3b), and the render-side composer (3b hardening). **Three call sites is the threshold the project needed to see before consolidating the primitive can be argued on evidence rather than on prediction.**

The local full-suite green (1,253 tests on the pre-merge PR #274 head `f895fb4`) and the byte-stable demo across the three hardening rounds confirm the discipline does not regress the legitimate path. The GitHub Actions check on the squash-merged head `b2b7a09` on `main` was SUCCESS.

## 3. Decision A — the H-track freeze lifts; H3 starts first

**The H-track freeze is lifted.** Per `docs/strategy/roadmap.md` §H-track ("frozen until M3 ships … lifted only at the M3 retro"), M3 has shipped (PR #274 merged at `b2b7a09`), and this retro is the gate. The freeze is lifted with one substantive ordering call:

**H3 (snapshot-primitive consolidation + negative-control automation) is the first H-track slice that should start.** The 3b hardening cycle confirms three call sites for the `util.types.isProxy` + own-data descriptor-snapshot discipline:

1. `src/workshop/proposal-durable-graph-write-execution.ts` — arming/contract/approval-packet artifacts at executor entry.
2. `src/workshop/durable-graph-snapshots-reader.ts` — each row at read boundary (with deep-freeze on the validated bundle as a 3b-specific addition).
3. `src/workshop/durable-state-render.ts` — root `readerResult`, every row, bundle, account_objects array, each account_object, and the decision artifact + decisions array + each decision record at compose entry.

H3's roadmap entry already names the target file (`src/safety/own-data-snapshot.ts`) and the "every doc-contract test ships a violating fixture it must fail on" framing. The 3b hardening cycle is exactly the case study that justifies the consolidation: three rounds of "the same shape of attack one layer deeper" is the failure mode a single primitive guards against. Beginning H3 with the executor + reader + render-side validator as its three call sites — and importing their refusal codes into a unified surface — would replace three independently-discovered hardening cycles with one place to put the next attack shape when it is found.

**H1 (approvals as typed data) should follow H3 and pair with M5a.** The roadmap's H1 entry already names "the first machine-checked approval should be M5a's"; that pairing remains correct. H1 lands when the M5a approval packet needs typed lifecycle counters that the M3 packet did not.

**H2 (capability registry + L0 mediation gate skeleton) was scheduled "between M3 retro and M4" per the roadmap; that scheduling is preserved.** H2 lands after H1 and before M4 begins. The first registered capability is the no-op echo server the roadmap names — proving the choke point on an effect that does nothing — not a real fetch.

**H4 (single guarded execution entrypoint), H5 (harness extraction assessment), and A3 (outward-facing read-only MCP server)** are unfrozen by the H-track lift but explicitly NOT next-up. H4 lands after H2 has a choke point worth guarding; H5 is a post-M5b decision point per the roadmap; A3 stays spec-only deferred until post-M5b per ADR 0003 A3.

The H-track freeze lift is therefore: **H3 next, then H1 (paired with M5a), then H2 (before M4), then H4/H5/A3 in their roadmap-recorded order.** The substantive H-track work that begins immediately is H3.

## 4. Decision B — M5a is next on the M-track; the M4-vs-M5a reordering challenge fails on its burden

**The default M3 → M5a → M4 → M5b sequence holds. The retro affirms M5a as the next M-track slice.**

The roadmap (`docs/strategy/roadmap.md` §Default sequence (burden of proof on reordering)) records the sequencing question as resolved into the default with burden of proof on the challenger to justify reordering. The substantive reasons that resolution holds at this retro:

- **The doctrine-loop is already real on curated sources at `b2b7a09`.** What M5a needs from the curated-source loop — a real ratified durable write, a real Workshop render from durable state, a real review-decision rejection visible beside it — exists in the committed demo at `fixtures/workshop/workshop-public-proposal-durable-state-render-demo.html`. M5a's job is to scale that loop to "a real-account-looking Workshop page rendered entirely from durable state without any system-acquisition path being exercised" — the curated-source capstone. That is a smaller risk surface than M4 introduces, and it lets the loop close before the new risk class lands.
- **M4 is its own capstone, with its own visible artifact and its own retro.** The roadmap is explicit ("not co-shipped with any M5 milestone"). Doing M4 first would mean adding the acquisition risk class (deterministic system-side fetcher, allowlist, sandboxing, provenance-tagging, adversarial injection corpus) before the doctrine loop has been proven end-to-end on a milestone-sized artifact. The 3b hardening cycle is recent evidence that risk surfaces have layers; landing M4 against an already-closed M5a loop means any M4 issue lands against an already-defended boundary, not against an undefended one.
- **The challenger's strongest argument — that the curated loop "would not suffice" as the doctrine-loop capstone — does not hold at this retro.** The 3a/3b artifact already contains a real ratified durable write with a real attribution chain and a real Workshop render. M5a does not need M4 to prove the loop; it needs to scale what M3 proved into a milestone-sized account artifact under curated sources. The acquisition risk class is a distinct contribution M4 makes; it is not the doctrine-loop proof.

Therefore: **M5a is the next M-track slice. M4 follows M5a. M5b follows M4. M6 follows M5b.** The H-track work concurrent with M5a is H1 (the M5a approval packet is the first machine-checked approval per the roadmap); the H-track work concurrent with M4 preparation is H2 (the L0 mediation gate skeleton). H3 is begun immediately and does not wait for either.

The reordering question is closed at this retro for the M3-to-M5a step; if a future retro revisits it, the challenger's burden remains "the alternative must explicitly justify why the curated loop proof would not suffice as the doctrine-loop capstone, or why the M4 risk class is required before the durable ratification path closes" (`docs/strategy/roadmap.md` §Default sequence (burden of proof on reordering)).

## 5. Scope — what this retro does NOT decide

This retro is explicit about its scope to avoid drift:

- **The roadmap update lands in this same PR.** Per `docs/strategy/roadmap.md`'s own maintenance rule ("When milestone status changes, update this file in the same PR as the change"), this PR flips the M3 row to ✅ shipped (anchored to `b2b7a09` and PR #274), records the H-track freeze lift in the H-track section, marks H3 as the next slice (with the three confirmed call sites named in its notes column), updates H1/H2/H4/H5/A3 status language to reflect the unfreeze and the queueing recorded in §3, and closes the M4-vs-M5a revisit language in §Operator decisions ledger #3 for the M3-to-M5a step. The pattern differs from the 3a retro on purpose: the 3a retro pre-dated the slice it gated (3b); this retro post-dates the slice it gates (M3), so the roadmap state actually changes here, and the maintenance rule requires the same-PR update.
- **It does not authorize M5a to begin.** M5a needs its own approval packet under the M5a contract. The pattern is the same one the durable graph-write slices used: contract → approval packet → arming → executor → render. M5a's contract is the first artifact M5a will produce.
- **It does not authorize H3 to begin without a slice plan.** H3 should begin with an explicit slice plan that names: the consolidated primitive's public API; the three call-site migrations (executor, reader, render-side validator); the negative-control automation framing for "every doc-contract test ships a violating fixture it must fail on"; and the regression suite that proves the three migrations do not regress any of the eleven enumerated reject paths recorded in the 3a retro §3 plus the 3b hardening-cycle additions.
- **It does not change the npm audit baseline finding posture.** The 3a retro acknowledged and correctly scoped that out of M3; the H-track freeze lift does not change that. A dedicated dependency-audit slice remains a separate consideration.
- **It does not lift any operator-direct gate.** The decisions in §3 and §4 are recommendations the operator may ratify or override. The retro's gating effect is that M5a planning is unblocked and H3 work is unblocked once this retro is committed on `main`; no work that this retro authorizes begins before the retro is merged.
